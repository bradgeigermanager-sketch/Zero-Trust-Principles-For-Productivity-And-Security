Research Paper: Reducing Security Debt and Complexity via Unified Zero Trust Policies

**Abstract**
As organizations accumulate heterogeneous technologies over time, security management becomes fragmented and costly—a phenomenon often termed "security debt." This paper investigates how transitioning to a Zero Trust architecture acts as a catalyst for simplifying security management. By centralizing identity-based policies and deprecating reliance on fragmented, location-based network rules, organizations can realize significant management efficiencies. This research highlights the administrative and fiscal benefits of ZT, demonstrating its utility as a framework for operational consolidation.

**Introduction**
Managing disparate firewall rules, guest network policies, and remote access servers is a significant administrative burden. Zero Trust offers a paradigm shift toward a single, unified policy control plane based on identity.

**Organizational Benefits**

* **Operational Simplicity:** ZT allows security teams to manage one set of identity-based policies that apply across the entire digital estate. This reduces the time spent on manual configuration and minimizes the risk of policy conflicts.
* **Troubleshooting Efficiency:** With comprehensive logging of every access attempt, IT teams gain a clear diagnostic trail, drastically reducing the time required to resolve "access denied" help desk tickets.

**Individual and Device-Level Interaction**
For the individual, this shift results in a consistent user experience. Whether an employee is at home, at a client site, or in the office, the access method and security experience remain uniform, leading to higher productivity and lower user frustration.

**Conclusion**
The adoption of Zero Trust is an opportunity to pay down security debt. By consolidating security management into a unified, identity-centric model, organizations can reduce administrative overhead, simplify support, and create a scalable architecture for long-term growth.
